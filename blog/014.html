Repairing a 68K Macintosh:
The Resurrection of a Centris 650

- Work Log -

This restoration is a work-in-progress!
I'll be updating the log as I go.

This mac is being worked on in my own time, and is not yet revived. I plan to update this page as progress is made, adding more photos and descriptions as needed.

30 Jul 2024 [Permanent link]
I recently came into ownership of a free Macintosh Centris 650. A guy living across the street had a yard sale, and I saw this Macintosh with a monitor, mouse, and keyboard inside of his garage. It wasn't intended to be part of the sale, but I still asked how much he wanted for it, and he told me I could have it for free and that it did not currently work.

Knowing nothing about old macs, I thought "Well, it can't be TOO bad, can it?" and decided to take it. The previous owner also told me that he had some writing saved on it, and I offered to try to rescue it for him; he agreed. So maybe, if the Quantum hard disk works when I get to that point, he'll be getting his old files back.

The physical condition of the mac looked "not bad," as far as I was concerned.

Macintosh Centris 650 computer, "Macintosh Color Display (M1212), Apple Desktop Bus mouse II,
Apple Keyboard II (SMK 'spring over membrane' key switches), and a big ol' box of floppy disks.
A little dirty, but not too bad!

…But sometimes, looks can be deceiving, as we will soon see!

Cleaning it Up

The first thing I always do when I get old electronics (or even secondhand non-electronics) is to clean them up. I've had a case before where the simple act of cleaning off the motherboard caused a seemingly dead Nintendo 64 to start working, and it's also good for my health to not be breathing in all that old garage dust.

Cleaning the Keyboard

I did the keyboard first, since if I got the mac working and still have to clean the keyboard I might put off cleaning the keyboard indefinitely. Taking it apart, the keyboard was no big deal. It was held together mostly by screws; compared to a modern Apple product, especially, it was quite straightforward. I popped the keycaps off, scrubbed them, and put them back on. Meanwhile, I soaked the case plastic in warm water to loosen the dirt stuck on it.

The legends on some keys are wearing off, but apart from that, the keyboard looks quite dashing.
It never ceases to amaze me what a difference just a simple scrubbing can make!

I then tested the keyboard out with an ADB to USB adapter, which I already had lying around on account of the Apple Extended Keyboard that I got back in 2018 or so. Everything works just like it should! The key feel isn't anything fantastic… It bottoms out a little harder than many regular rubber dome boards, but it still uses buckling rubber sleeves for tactile feedback so some of the "rubber dome" feel is still definitely present. One nice thing is that the key doesn't quite have to bottom out to register… But my Apple Extended board from 1987 or so still feels better so I'll probably use that if I can get the computer working.

Cleaning the Computer

I skipped the monitor and mouse for now; I've done the mouse but I didn't take any pictures yet. I'll maybe come back to it later. The monitor still needs cleaning as of this writing, but I'm fairly confident that it will not be too bad.

I next took the case apart for the Centris itself. It was quite dusty inside… who knows how long it sat in that garage.

My initial view upon opening the case. Tons of dust!– The Motorola 68040 CPU is under the heat sink.

One good thing I noticed was a lack of aluminum electrolytic surface mount capacitors; Apple seems to have used tantalum caps, which have much better characteristics… except that when they fail they like to catch fire. But at least they don't tend to leak out liquid capacitor electrolyte (an acid) all over everything. So I've got that going for me, which is nice.

There was a thick layer of dust on the motherboard, but that's not in and of itself a deal breaker. It did get me hoping that maybe I'd just have to give the board a good cleaning and it might clear some short, letting the computer start working.

However, this notion was quickly abandoned, when I removed the hard drive and floppy drive mounting plate… and found the stuff that my nightmares are made of.

"Abandon all hope, ye who enter here."

Yes, the real-time clock battery had exploded. Or leaked very, very badly and somehow flung acid out so that it rained over part of the board.

I'd been lucky so far; I'd only seen bad battery leakage once before in my life, when it had ruined my Amiga 500's RAM expansion board.

My Commodore Amiga A501 memory expansion board - a victim of clock NiCad battery leakage.

In that case, I was able to sidestep the issue, since the expansion card was enclosed in sheet metal and the battery was not on the motherboard itself. The Amiga also had some unused spots on the main board that I was able to solder RAM into, totally obviating the need for the expansion slot except for the real-time clock (which I can live without or make my own modification for). Even the leakage in that, though, was nothing compared to the carnage I was witnesing here.

The battery had left a soupy mixture of rust and lithium-based battery acid that was destroying everything in its path. It wasn't a slow trickle followed by creeping corrosion in the traces; it was a dam bursting open and flooding the poor unsuspecting village below.

To many, this would be a complete and instant game-over. I still as of writing this am not certain that I can save this computer. But darn it, I got this for free! - So I have to try. If I can get it going, I can both have a 68040-based mac and also run NetBSD on it!– For much cheaper than any Amiga capable of doing the same. And even if I end up giving up and just replacing a motherboard, it will cost less than buying an A4000.

Rescuing a Battery-Bombed Macintosh

Since I was determined to save this thing, regardless of how much it clearly wanted to be dead, I began by trying to clean up the flooded "canals" of battery acid. The first step was removing the battery. I then used dry cotton swabs to try to mop up the battery acid that was still in liquid form. Then, i soaked the area in isopropyl alcohol.

Surprisingly, the silkscreen for the board seems fully intact despite the massive flooding.

Now let's take a moment to assess what might be damaged.

The area immediately around the battery contains the real time clock and PRAM, which are both inside the "VLSI" chip with pins on all four sides. It also where Apple decided it'd be good to solder down all of the system's built-in main RAM. All sixteen of those Motorola "MCM44400N80" chips constitute the computer's main working memory. This can be further extended by adding sticks in the black RAM slots to the left of these chips. One of those slots was also splashed by battery acid and Needs to be removed. The VRAM slots (an uncommon 68-pin SIMM variant) are also attacked by acid and need to be taken out. As we will eventually see, this will be quite tricky due to the solder itself being degraded by the battery acid.

In a way, this is actually a kind of the best part of the board to get destroyed, because it means the only custom chip that I need to worry about being destroyed is the PRAM/RTC hybrid chip. The rest is commodity dynamic RAM (DRAM), which even though it's been out of production for many years is still easy to obtain online. It's the same kinds of chips that were used in many IBM PC compatibles and even non-IBM compatibles used from that period. In fact, I think my Epson PC-486GR uses the same or very similar chips, and I know for sure that my friend's PC-9821Ce2 and Cs2 computers use them, too. So soldering them all down without a hot air station will actually be a trickier problem than getting the chips.

I saw an ebay listing for forty compatible RAM chips from Toshiba in their original factory packaging for $25, for instance. Not too bad at all, even on a shoestring budget. This is "Fast Page Mode" (FPM) DRAM; do not get EDO DRAM or it will likely not work.

Miraculously, in addition to the silkscreen on the board remaining intact, even the traces still look okay and are visible underneath the solder-resist, which is the green film layer that protects the traces during the soldering process. Often with battery leakage, corrosion creeps down below the solder resist and wipes the traces out from beneath.

The next step was using alcohol-drenched cotton swabs to clean up whatever mess was still left.

It's looking much better now…
but battery acid still lurks underneath the RAM slots and the sixteen DRAM chips themselves.

Since there was still battery acid underneath the chips, and the RAM slots for that matter, I needed to figure out what to do about that. I knew there was probably no way I could avoid desoldering the chips to properly clean under them. This is quite problematic– primarily because I have no hot air station (and surface mount desoldering with an iron and wick is not fun at all). The other issue is that soldering them back down without the correct iron tip is a challenge to say the least, and I'm about to ruin my only "proper" iron tip doing something else to this Macintosh (we will probably get to that soon).

But before I desoldered the chips, there were two last things I wanted to try to clean up the board. One was removing the battery holder so I could clean up under it; the other was something sort of unorthodox but which I had a feeling could work decently.

There was quite a lot of battery residue hiding under the battery holder itself.
One of the pins on the holder had actually been eaten through!!

With that removed, I did a little scrubbing with cotton swabs again, and then was ready to try my next trick… something I had never done before.

A dental water flosser seemed like a good way to attack the smaller areas swabs can't reach.

Guess what? it worked great. I soaked it in alcohol again after using the flosser on it, since the alcohol can help the water to evaporate faster, and then let the board dry off with a fan blowing air on it overnight. When I came back the next day, it looked markedly better.

There's almost no visible acid residue left. The gaps between RAM pins also look better,
and most or all of the dust is gone, too! A great success. 10/10, highly recommended.

Next, I had to stop beating around the bush and try to remove all of the chips. I have no hot air station (which just blows super hot air on the board to melt the solder on all the pins of a chip at once to allow removal), so I had to improvise.

This is the kind of job I had for a very long time said "I'll do it later when I have the right tools." I have a SNES that I think I can fix if I can obtain the correct ones, for instance. And an NEC PC-9801-86 sound card (with a Yamaha YM2608 Frequency Modulated (FM) synthesizer chip). But since tools were not forthcoming, I decided I needed to bite the bullet on this one.

A friend of mine with similar interests living in France that I spoke with on both IRC and Fediverse (mastodon et al) told me to try flooding one side of the chip with solder and melting it all, then bending that side of the chip up and doing the other.

It seemed as good an idea as any, although it's complicated by the fact that there's a large gap between pins 5 and "9" (I'd call it "pin 6," but the datasheet and schematic both skip pins 6, 7, and 8). To avoid this, I flooded each side independently and used my second soldering iron to melt the second set of pins, prying up with both irons.

This is something you should try to avoid on boards that might already have pads and traces weakened by battery acid, but because this is RAM specifically it is separated out into "buses" that make it so the nearby chips share a lot of wiring with each other and most signals can easily be grabbed from them.

Actually, i also discovered that three of the chips had already had each and every pin and almost every pad on one side eaten away before I began, which made things a little easier for desoldering.

Lots of battery acid under there, too! Good thing I'm taking these off to clean…

In any case, my technique basically worked. I think most or all of the damaged pins were already gone due to battery acid; all of the five chips that have all pads intact after removal are the ones that the acid didn't land on or otherwise touch.

If anyone's wondering though, yes, this means just a water flosser is unlikely to be able to get all the acid off!

With three chips lifted up on one side, that's 30 pins I no longer need to worry about further damaging. :\

After all of them are removed, it's time for a damage assessment. Five chips' worth of pads are completely unscathed. Apple's Quadra 800 and Centris 650 used the same motherboard, and the schematic for them shows that some configurations of Centris 650 shipped with only eight soldered-down RAM chips. All five of the undamaged chip pads are on the correct side for a 4MB factory configuration, mercifully! - so I might only solder eight chips back down to save myself some work and at least verify that the system still can have a pulse.

It looks much cleaner now, although it's going to be a headache wiring it all back up. And the corroded SIMM slots still need removed for cleaning.
